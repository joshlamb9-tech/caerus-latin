```html
<h1>The Subjunctive</h1>
<p class="page-intro">The subjunctive is a <em>mood</em>, not a tense. It expresses actions that are uncertain, intended, or indirect. In GCSE Latin you need to recognise and translate subjunctives in purpose clauses, result clauses, indirect commands, <em>cum</em> clauses, and indirect questions.</p>

<section id="formation">
  <h2>How the Subjunctive is Formed</h2>
  <h3>Present subjunctive (active)</h3>
  <p>The present subjunctive is formed by changing the stem vowel. A useful mnemonic: <strong>"We are subj."</strong> → 1st conj. uses -e- (amem); others use -a- (moneam, ducam, audiam).</p>
  <h3>Imperfect subjunctive</h3>
  <p>The imperfect subjunctive = <strong>present infinitive + personal endings</strong>. For amō: amāre → amārem, amārēs, amāret... This is the easiest to form: if you know the infinitive, you know the imperfect subjunctive.</p>
  <h3>Pluperfect subjunctive</h3>
  <p>The pluperfect subjunctive = <strong>perfect stem + -isse- + personal endings</strong>. For amō: amāv- → amāvissem, amāvissēs, amāvisset...</p>
  <h3>Passive forms</h3>
  <p>Subjunctive passive forms follow the same pattern: replace the active personal endings with passive endings (-r, -ris, -tur, -mur, -minī, -ntur). The pluperfect passive subjunctive is compound: PPP + essem/essēs/esset/essēmus/essētis/essent.</p>
</section>

<section id="sequence-of-tenses">
  <h2>Sequence of Tenses</h2>
  <p>Which subjunctive tense you use depends on the tense of the main verb:</p>
  <table class="grammar-table">
    <thead><tr><th>Main verb tense</th><th>Sequence</th><th>Subj. tense used</th></tr></thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td>Present or future</td><td>Primary</td><td>Present subjunctive</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Past (imperfect, perfect, pluperfect)</td><td>Secondary</td><td>Imperfect subjunctive</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
  <p><em>Mīlitēs pugnant ut vincant.</em> — The soldiers are fighting in order to win. (present main → present subj.)</p>
  <p><em>Mīlitēs pugnābant ut vincērent.</em> — The soldiers were fighting in order to win. (imperfect main → imperfect subj.)</p>
  <p>The pluperfect subjunctive appears in secondary sequence when the subordinate action happened <em>before</em> the main verb: <em>cum hostēs advenissent, cīvēs fugerunt.</em> (When the enemy had arrived, the citizens fled.)</p>
</section>

<section id="subjunctive-tables">

</section>

<div class="exam-tip">

Look for the trigger word: <em>ut</em> (purpose/result/indirect command), <em>cum</em> (when/although/since), <em>nē</em> (negative purpose). Then translate the subjunctive as "would", "might", or the appropriate English construction — not literally.

<h1>Deponent Verbs</h1>
<p class="page-intro">Deponent verbs are passive in form but active in meaning. They have no active voice forms at all. You need to recognise them and translate them as active — do not be misled by the passive endings.</p>

<section id="what-are-deponents">
  <h2>What Are Deponents?</h2>
  <p>A deponent verb looks passive but means something active. The key signs:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>The present ends in <strong>-or</strong> (1st person singular): <em>loquor</em> = I speak</li>
    <li>The infinitive ends in <strong>-ī</strong>: <em>loquī</em> = to speak</li>
    <li>The perfect uses the perfect passive participle + <em>sum</em>: <em>locūtus sum</em> = I spoke (not "I was spoken")</li>
  </ul>
  <h3>Common GCSE deponent verbs</h3>
  <table class="grammar-table">
    <thead><tr><th>Verb</th><th>Infinitive</th><th>Perfect</th><th>Meaning</th></tr></thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td><em>loquor</em></td><td><em>loquī</em></td><td><em>locūtus sum</em></td><td>speak</td></tr>
      <tr><td><em>sequor</em></td><td><em>sequī</em></td><td><em>secūtus sum</em></td><td>follow</td></tr>
      <tr><td><em>patior</em></td><td><em>patī</em></td><td><em>passus sum</em></td><td>suffer, allow</td></tr>
      <tr><td><em>hortor</em></td><td><em>hortārī</em></td><td><em>hortātus sum</em></td><td>encourage</td></tr>
      <tr><td><em>moror</em></td><td><em>morārī</em></td><td><em>morātus sum</em></td><td>delay, linger</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</section>

<section id="deponent-paradigm">
  <h2>Paradigm: loquor (to speak)</h2>
  <p><em>loquor</em> is a 3rd conjugation deponent. Its forms look passive but translate actively.</p>
  <div id="deponent-tables"></div>
</section>

<section id="parsing">
  <h2>Parsing Deponents</h2>
  <p>When you parse a deponent form, give: person, number, tense, voice ("deponent — active meaning"), mood, and meaning.</p>
  <p>Example: <em>loquitur</em> → 3rd person singular, present, deponent (passive form/active meaning), indicative = <strong>he/she speaks</strong>.</p>
  <p>Example: <em>secūtī sunt</em> → 3rd person plural, perfect, deponent = <strong>they followed</strong>.</p>
</section>

<div class="exam-tip">
  <strong>Exam tip:</strong> The biggest deponent trap in translation is the perfect: <em>locūtus est</em> looks like "he was spoken" (perfect passive) but means "he spoke". Always check whether a verb is listed as a deponent in the vocabulary list. If it is, the -us sum / -a est / -ī sunt forms are perfect active meanings, not passive.
</div>

        '1st': ['I speak','we speak'],

          + '<td><em>' + t.singular[p] + '</em></td>'
