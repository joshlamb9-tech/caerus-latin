```html
<h1>The Gerund and Gerundive</h1>
<p class="page-intro">The <strong>gerund</strong> is a verbal noun — it names the action of a verb. The <strong>gerundive</strong> is a verbal adjective — it agrees with a noun and often expresses obligation. Both are formed from the verb stem and are common in J282 unseen texts.</p>

<section id="gerund-explanation">
  <h2>The Gerund</h2>
  <p>The gerund is a <strong>2nd-declension neuter noun</strong> used only in the singular, and only in the oblique cases (acc, gen, dat, abl). It is never used in the nominative — use the infinitive instead.</p>

  <h3>Formation</h3>
  <p>Add <strong>-ndum</strong> (acc), <strong>-ndī</strong> (gen), <strong>-ndō</strong> (dat/abl) to the present stem:</p>

  <table class="grammar-table">
    <thead>
      <tr><th>Case</th><th>Ending</th><th>1st conj. (amō)</th><th>2nd conj. (moneō)</th><th>3rd conj. (regō)</th><th>4th conj. (audiō)</th></tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td>Acc.</td><td>-ndum</td><td><em>amandum</em></td><td><em>monendum</em></td><td><em>regendum</em></td><td><em>audiendum</em></td></tr>
      <tr><td>Gen.</td><td>-ndī</td><td><em>amandī</em></td><td><em>monendī</em></td><td><em>regendī</em></td><td><em>audiendī</em></td></tr>
      <tr><td>Dat.</td><td>-ndō</td><td><em>amandō</em></td><td><em>monendō</em></td><td><em>regendō</em></td><td><em>audiendō</em></td></tr>
      <tr><td>Abl.</td><td>-ndō</td><td><em>amandō</em></td><td><em>monendō</em></td><td><em>regendō</em></td><td><em>audiendō</em></td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

  <h3>Worked Examples</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><em>Cupidus <strong>pugnandī</strong> erat.</em> — He was eager <strong>for fighting</strong>. (gen after cupidus)</li>
    <li><em>Veniērunt ad <strong>pugnandum</strong>.</em> — They came <strong>to fight</strong>. (acc after ad)</li>
    <li><em><strong>Legendo</strong> sapientia augētur.</em> — By <strong>reading</strong>, wisdom is increased. (abl of means)</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section id="gerundive-explanation">
  <h2>The Gerundive</h2>
  <p>The gerundive is a <strong>verbal adjective</strong> (-andus/-anda/-andum for 1st conj., -endus/-enda/-endum for 2nd–4th). It agrees with a noun in case, number, and gender.</p>

  <h3>Gerundive of Obligation</h3>
  <p>When used with a form of <em>sum</em>, the gerundive expresses <strong>obligation or necessity</strong>: "X must be done" / "X ought to be done".</p>
  <ul>
    <li><em>Liber <strong>legendus est</strong>.</em> — The book <strong>must be read</strong>.</li>
    <li><em>Pāx <strong>petenda est</strong>.</em> — Peace <strong>must be sought</strong>.</li>
    <li><em>Hostēs <strong>vincendī sunt</strong>.</em> — The enemy <strong>must be conquered</strong>.</li>
  </ul>

  <h3>Gerundive Replacing Gerund + Object</h3>
  <p>When a gerund would take a direct object, Latin usually replaces it with a gerundive agreeing with the object:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>gerund + object: <em>ad urbem <strong>capiendam</strong></em> — for the purpose of capturing the city</li>
    <li>(instead of: <em>ad capiendum urbem</em>)</li>
  </ul>
</section>
```
