**purpose.html** (`gcse/syntax/purpose.html`):
- Intro: "A purpose clause expresses the aim or intention behind an action. In Latin, it is introduced by ut (positive) or nē (negative) + a subjunctive verb."
- Explanation:
  - Positive purpose: main verb + ut + subjunctive — "in order to / so that [subject] may verb"
  - Negative purpose: main verb + nē + subjunctive — "in order not to / so that [subject] may not verb"
  - Alternative: ad + gerund or gerundive (ad urbem aedificandam = for the purpose of building a city)
  - Tense: present subjunctive (primary sequence), imperfect subjunctive (secondary sequence) — link to sequence-of-tenses.html.
- Worked examples (3+):
  1. vēnit ut rēgem vidēret. — He came in order to see the king. (secondary: imperfect subjunctive)
  2. fūgērunt nē caperentur. — They fled so that they would not be captured.
  3. mīlitēs misit ut urbem dēfenderent. — He sent soldiers to defend the city.
  4. laborāmus ut discāmus. — We work in order to learn. (primary: present subjunctive)
- Comparison table (title "Purpose vs Result Clauses"):
  | Feature | Purpose Clause | Result Clause |
  | Introducer | ut / nē | ut (always positive after tam/ita/adeo) |
  | Clue in main clause | verb of motion or intention | tam, ita, adeō, tantus, tot |
  | Question it answers | Why? (aim) | What happened? (outcome) |
  | Subjunctive tense | follows sequence | follows sequence |
  | Example | vēnit ut vidēret | tam fortis erat ut nēmō eum vinceret |
- Exam tip: "Purpose and result both use ut + subjunctive — the distinction is the signal in the main clause. Result always has tam, ita, adeō, or tantus pointing forward to the ut clause. Purpose does not."
- Sequence of tenses link: "The subjunctive tense follows <a href='sequence-of-tenses.html'>sequence of tenses</a>."
- See also: result.html, indirect-commands.html
- Exercise fetch: `fetch('../../data/exercises/gcse-purpose.json')`

**result.html** (`gcse/syntax/result.html`):
- Intro: "A result clause describes the consequence of an action. It is introduced by ut (positive) or ut nōn (negative) + a subjunctive verb, always with an intensifying word in the main clause."
- Explanation:
  - Intensifying words in main clause: tam (so), ita (so), adeō (to such an extent), tantus (so great), tot (so many).
  - The ut clause then states the result: "so [adj/adv] that..."
  - Negative result: ut nōn + subjunctive — "so X that not..."
  - Distinguishing from purpose: result has tam/ita/adeō/tantus; purpose does not.
- Worked examples (3+):
  1. tam fortis erat ut nēmō eum vincere posset. — He was so brave that no one was able to defeat him.
  2. ita festīnāvit ut caderet. — He hurried so much that he fell.
  3. tantus timor erat ut omnēs fugerent. — There was such great fear that everyone fled.
  4. tot mīlitēs erant ut numerārī nōn possent. — There were so many soldiers that they could not be counted.
- Comparison table: same table as purpose.html (Purpose vs Result Clauses) — copy verbatim.
- Exam tip: "Look for tam, ita, adeō, tantus, or tot in the main clause — that is your signal that a result clause is coming. If you see ut + subjunctive with no intensifying word in the main clause, it is more likely a purpose clause or indirect command."
- Sequence of tenses link as above.
- See also: purpose.html, fearing.html
- Exercise fetch: `fetch('../../data/exercises/gcse-result.json')`

**fearing.html** (`gcse/syntax/fearing.html`):
- Intro: "A fearing clause follows a verb of fearing (timeō, vereor, metuō) and reports what someone fears will or will not happen. The logic of nē and ut is reversed from other constructions."
- Explanation:
  - After a fear verb: nē + subjunctive = positive fear (I fear THAT something WILL happen)
  - After a fear verb: ut + subjunctive = negative fear (I fear THAT something WILL NOT happen)
  - Memory aid: with fear verbs, nē = "that" (not "not to"); ut = "that not"
  - Why? Because the original nē was a wish ("may it not happen") — the speaker fears the bad thing will occur.
- Worked examples (3+):
  1. timeō nē hostēs veniant. — I fear that the enemy will come.
  2. verēbār nē caperēmur. — I was afraid that we would be captured.
  3. timēbat ut amīcī redirent. — He was afraid that his friends would not return.
  4. metuō nē hoc verum sit. — I fear that this is true.
- Exam tip: "With fearing verbs, nē means 'that' (positive fear) and ut means 'that not' (negative fear). This is the opposite of their meaning in purpose clauses. The fear verb in the main clause is your first signal — once you spot timeō, vereor, or metuō, the nē/ut logic switches."
- See also: purpose.html, indirect-commands.html
- Exercise fetch: `fetch('../../data/exercises/gcse-fearing.json')`

**temporal-causal-concessive.html** (`gcse/syntax/temporal-causal-concessive.html`):
- Intro: "Latin uses several conjunctions to express time, cause, and concession. The conjunction cum is especially versatile — its meaning depends on the mood of its verb."
- Explanation sections (3 sub-sections):
  1. Temporal clauses: cum + indicative = when/whenever; dum + indicative = while; postquam + perfect indicative = after; ubi + perfect indicative = when.
  2. Causal clauses: cum + subjunctive (imperfect or pluperfect) can mean since/because; quod/quia + indicative = because (stating a fact).
  3. Concessive clauses: quamquam + indicative = although (accepted fact); cum + subjunctive (imperfect or pluperfect) = although (writer's viewpoint); tamen often in main clause.
- Worked examples (3+ across types):
  1. cum in forō stāret, clāmōrem audīvit. — When he was standing in the forum, he heard a shout. (cum + imperfect indicative = temporal: when)
  2. cum servus fessus esset, laborāre coāctus est. — Although/Since the slave was tired, he was forced to work. (cum + imperfect subjunctive = causal or concessive; context determines)
  3. quamquam perīculum erat, mīlitēs nōn fūgērunt. — Although there was danger, the soldiers did not flee. (quamquam + indicative = concessive)
  4. dum cēnat, nuntius advēnit. — While he was dining, a messenger arrived. (dum + indicative)
- Comparison table (title "Temporal, Causal, and Concessive Clauses"):
  | Signal | Type | Mood | Translation |
  | cum + indicative | Temporal | Indicative | when / whenever |
  | cum + subjunctive (imperfect/pluperfect) | Causal or Concessive | Subjunctive | since/because OR although |
  | quamquam | Concessive | Indicative | although |
  | dum | Temporal | Indicative (present) | while |
  | quod / quia | Causal | Indicative | because |
- Exam tip: "When you see cum + subjunctive, look for tamen in the main clause — tamen signals concession (although). Without tamen, cum + subjunctive is more likely causal (since). Context and logic are your final arbiter."
- Sequence of tenses link: for cum + subjunctive constructions.
- See also: conditionals.html, ablative-absolute.html
- Exercise fetch: `fetch('../../data/exercises/gcse-temporal-causal-concessive.json')`

**conditionals.html** (`gcse/syntax/conditionals.html`):
- Intro: "Conditional sentences express 'if X, then Y'. Latin distinguishes between open conditions (something that may or may not be true) and unfulfilled conditions (something that is contrary to fact)."
- Explanation sections:
  1. Open (simple) conditional: si/nisi + indicative in both clauses. States a straightforward condition. E.g. si hoc dīcis, errās. — If you say this, you are wrong.
  2. Unfulfilled conditional (present): si/nisi + imperfect subjunctive in both clauses. Contrary to present fact. E.g. si hoc dīcerēs, errārēs. — If you were saying this (but you are not), you would be wrong.
  3. Unfulfilled conditional (past): si/nisi + pluperfect subjunctive in both clauses. Contrary to past fact. E.g. si hoc dīxissēs, errāvissēs. — If you had said this (but you did not), you would have been wrong.
- Worked examples (3+):
  1. si labōrās, discis. — If you work, you learn. (open: present indicative)
  2. si plūs labōrārēs, plūs discerēs. — If you worked more (but you don't), you would learn more. (unfulfilled present: imperfect subjunctive)
  3. si mīlitēs fortius pugnāvissent, vīcissent. — If the soldiers had fought more bravely (but they didn't), they would have won. (unfulfilled past: pluperfect subjunctive)
  4. nisi advenissent, omnia perdita essent. — If they had not arrived, everything would have been lost.
- Comparison table (title "Types of Conditional"):
  | Type | si clause | Main clause | Meaning |
  | Open | indicative | indicative | genuine if/then |
  | Unfulfilled (present) | imperfect subjunctive | imperfect subjunctive | if X were happening (but it isn't)... |
  | Unfulfilled (past) | pluperfect subjunctive | pluperfect subjunctive | if X had happened (but it didn't)... |
- Exam tip: "Imperfect subjunctive in both halves = present unfulfilled (something contrary to what is happening now). Pluperfect subjunctive in both halves = past unfulfilled (something contrary to what happened). Indicative in both halves = open conditional — no special conditions attached."
- See also: temporal-causal-concessive.html, result.html

**sequence-of-tenses.html** (`gcse/syntax/sequence-of-tenses.html`):

This page is auth-gated (same pattern as all construction pages). It has NO exercises — it is a reference page only.

Structure:
- Title: "Sequence of Tenses — GCSE Syntax — Caerus Latin"
- h1: "Sequence of Tenses"
- page-intro paragraph: "When a Latin subjunctive clause depends on a main verb, the tense of the subjunctive must match the tense of the main verb. This is called sequence of tenses. It applies to purpose clauses, result clauses, indirect commands, indirect questions, and temporal/causal/concessive clauses."
- Section "Primary and Secondary Sequence":
  - Explanation: Latin tenses divide into two groups. Primary tenses (present, future, future perfect) call for a primary subjunctive (present or perfect). Secondary tenses (imperfect, perfect, pluperfect) call for a secondary subjunctive (imperfect or pluperfect).
  - Main table (title "Sequence of Tenses"):
    | Main verb tense | Sequence | Subjunctive for same-time action | Subjunctive for prior action |
    | Present | Primary | Present subjunctive | Perfect subjunctive |
    | Future | Primary | Present subjunctive | Perfect subjunctive |
    | Future perfect | Primary | Present subjunctive | Perfect subjunctive |
    | Imperfect | Secondary | Imperfect subjunctive | Pluperfect subjunctive |
    | Perfect | Secondary | Imperfect subjunctive | Pluperfect subjunctive |
    | Pluperfect | Secondary | Imperfect subjunctive | Pluperfect subjunctive |
- Section "Examples":
  - Primary (present main verb + purpose): venit ut rēgem videat. — He comes to see the king. (present subjunctive)
  - Secondary (imperfect main verb + purpose): vēnit ut rēgem vidēret. — He came to see the king. (imperfect subjunctive)
  - Secondary (perfect main verb + indirect question, prior action): rogāvī quis id fēcisset. — I asked who had done it. (pluperfect subjunctive — prior to the asking)
- Exam tip: "A quick rule of thumb: if the main verb ends in -t or is present/future, you are in primary sequence (present or perfect subjunctive). If the main verb is past (any past tense), you are in secondary sequence (imperfect or pluperfect subjunctive)."
- Section "See also": links to purpose.html, result.html, indirect-commands.html, indirect-questions.html, temporal-causal-concessive.html, conditionals.html.
