- Content structure:
  - h1: "The PETE Method"
  - Intro paragraph: "PETE is a structured approach for answering literature questions in OCR J282. Each paragraph of your response should follow these four steps."
  - Four sections, each with h2 and explanation:
    - **P — Point**: State your argument clearly. "The author creates a sense of danger in this passage." Explain this is the topic sentence that answers the question directly.
    - **E — Evidence**: Quote the Latin text. Use the exact Latin words from the passage. Example: "The poet writes 'magnō cum timōre currēbant' (line 3)." Always include the line reference.
    - **T — Translation**: Translate the quoted Latin. "They were running with great fear." The examiner needs to see you understand what the Latin means.
    - **E — Explanation**: Explain HOW the evidence supports your point. "The ablative phrase 'magnō cum timōre' emphasises the intensity of their fear, while the imperfect 'currēbant' suggests continuous, panicked movement." This is where you analyse the language — word choice, word order, tense, literary devices.
  - Exam tip box (class="exam-tip", gold left border): "In a 10-mark question, aim for 2-3 PETE paragraphs. Each paragraph scores up to 3-4 marks. Quality of explanation matters more than quantity of points."

  - Cross-reference link to mangoes.html: "Use MANGOES to identify literary devices for your Explanation step."

**gcse/literature/mangoes.html — MANGOES Literary Devices:**
- Title: "MANGOES Literary Devices &mdash; GCSE Literature &mdash; Caerus Latin"
- Meta description: "MANGOES mnemonic for Latin literary devices — Metaphor, Alliteration, Noun clusters, Graphic language, Onomatopoeia, Enjambment, Simile"
- Auth guard: hard-redirect (same pattern as pete.html)
- Nav: same Literature nav group as pete.html
- Content structure:
  - h1: "MANGOES — Literary Devices"
  - Intro paragraph: "MANGOES is a mnemonic for the seven key literary devices you need to identify and analyse in OCR J282 literature questions."
  - Seven sections, each with h2, definition, Latin example, and analysis tip:
    - **M — Metaphor**: Describing something as if it were something else (without "like" or "as"). Latin example: "Rōma est caput mundī" (Rome is the head of the world). Analysis: "The metaphor 'caput' elevates Rome from a city to the controlling mind of civilisation."
    - **A — Alliteration**: Repetition of initial consonant sounds. Latin example: "Saepe senex sēcrētō suspīrābat" (Often the old man sighed secretly). Analysis: "The repeated 's' sounds create a hushed, secretive atmosphere."
    - **N — Noun clusters**: Groups of nouns listed together for cumulative effect. Latin example: "Flammae, clāmōrēs, lacrimae, sanguis — omnia terrēbant" (Flames, shouts, tears, blood — everything terrified). Analysis: "The four-noun asyndeton (no conjunctions) creates a rapid, overwhelming catalogue of horrors."
    - **G — Graphic / vivid language**: Specific, visual, sensory vocabulary. Latin example: "Gladius per pectus trānsīit; sanguis āter effluēbat" (The sword passed through his chest; dark blood flowed out). Analysis: "The adjective 'āter' (dark/black) makes the blood visceral and unsettling — more vivid than simply 'sanguis'."
    - **O — Onomatopoeia**: Words that sound like what they describe. Latin example: "Unda fragōsa ruēbat" (The crashing wave rushed down). Analysis: "'Fragōsa' mimics the crash and break of waves — the hard 'g' and rolling 'r' are audibly violent."
    - **E — Enjambment**: A sentence or clause running over a line break. Latin example: "...et magnā / vōce clāmāvit" (and with a great voice he shouted — where the line breaks after 'magnā'). Analysis: "The enjambment delays the verb 'clāmāvit', creating suspense and mirroring the intake of breath before shouting."
    - **S — Simile**: A comparison using "like" or "as" (in Latin: velut, sīcut, tamquam, quasi). Latin example: "Mīlitēs velut leōnēs pugnābant" (The soldiers were fighting like lions). Analysis: "The simile 'velut leōnēs' immediately conveys ferocity and bravery without lengthy description."
  - Exam tip box: "You do not need to find all seven devices in every passage. Identify 2-3 that are genuinely present and analyse them in depth using PETE. Examiners reward quality of analysis over quantity of devices spotted."
  - Cross-reference link to pete.html: "Use the PETE method to structure your analysis of each device."

- Title: "Essay Skills &mdash; GCSE Literature &mdash; Caerus Latin"
- Meta description: "10-mark essay skills for OCR J282 — writing template, paragraph structure, and model answer with examiner commentary"
- Auth guard: hard-redirect (same pattern)

**Sections:**

1. **h1: "10-Mark Essay Skills"**
   - Intro: "The 10-mark question in J282 asks you to analyse how an author achieves a particular effect. You typically have 20 minutes and should write 2-3 PETE paragraphs."

2. **h2: "Writing Template"**
   - Present a structured template in a styled box (use a div with border: 2px solid #c9a84c, background: #fdf8ee, padding: 1.25rem, border-radius: 6px):
   - Template content (use an ordered list):
     1. **Opening sentence** — Answer the question directly. "The author creates a sense of [effect] through [technique 1] and [technique 2]."
     2. **PETE Paragraph 1** — Your strongest point. Quote Latin, translate, explain how the language creates the effect.
     3. **PETE Paragraph 2** — A second point, ideally about a different technique (e.g., if paragraph 1 was word choice, paragraph 2 could be word order or imagery).
     4. **PETE Paragraph 3 (if time)** — A third point OR develop paragraph 2 further. Do not start a new point you cannot finish.
     5. **Brief conclusion (optional)** — One sentence linking your points. "Overall, the author combines [technique 1] and [technique 2] to create a powerful sense of [effect]."

3. **h2: "Mark Scheme: What Examiners Want"**
   - Table (class="grammar-table") with two columns: Mark Range | Description
     - 9-10: "Detailed, perceptive analysis. Accurate Latin quotation with precise translation. Sophisticated explanation of how language creates effects."
     - 7-8: "Sound analysis with relevant Latin evidence. Translation mostly accurate. Clear explanation of effects, though less nuanced."
     - 5-6: "Some valid points with Latin evidence. Translation may have minor errors. Explanation tends toward description rather than analysis."
     - 3-4: "Basic points, limited Latin evidence. Translation may be inaccurate. Little analysis of how language creates effects."
     - 1-2: "Very limited response. Little or no Latin evidence. Description rather than analysis."

4. **h2: "Model Answer"**
   - Question prompt in a styled box: "How does the author make this passage exciting? Support your answer with reference to the Latin text. [10 marks]"
   - Context line: "Passage: A Roman general rallies his troops before battle."
   - Latin excerpt (styled in italic, with line numbers):
     - (1) "Mīlitēs!" clāmāvit dux magnā vōce. "Hostēs
     - (2) ante portās stant. Nōlīte timēre! Patria
     - (3) vestra vōs vocat. Gladiōs capite et mēcum
     - (4) in proelium currite — aut vincēmus aut
     - (5) cum glōriā cadēmus!"
   - Model answer (3 PETE paragraphs):
     - **Paragraph 1**: "The author creates immediate excitement through the general's dramatic opening. The direct speech begins with the vocative 'Mīlitēs!' (line 1), placed emphatically at the start. The verb 'clāmāvit' (he shouted, line 1) combined with the ablative phrase 'magnā vōce' (with a great voice) conveys urgency and volume. The short, punchy exclamation creates a sudden, energetic opening that places the reader in the scene."
     - **Paragraph 2**: "The author builds tension by presenting the danger directly. 'Hostēs ante portās stant' (The enemy stand before the gates, lines 1-2) uses the present tense 'stant' to make the threat feel immediate rather than distant. The enjambment of 'Hostēs' at the end of line 1 running into line 2 creates a sense of the enemy pressing forward, while the imperative 'Nōlīte timēre!' (Do not be afraid!, line 2) acknowledges the soldiers' fear while commanding courage."
     - **Paragraph 3**: "Finally, the author creates a climactic ending through the rhetorical alternative 'aut vincēmus aut... cadēmus' (either we shall conquer or we shall fall, lines 4-5). The parallel structure of 'aut...aut' presents only two outcomes — victory or glorious death — eliminating retreat as an option. The phrase 'cum glōriā' (with glory, line 5) transforms even death into something noble, leaving the reader with a rousing, heroic conclusion."
   - Examiner commentary box (class="exam-tip"): "This answer would score 9-10 marks. Each paragraph follows PETE clearly. The student quotes Latin accurately with line references, translates precisely, and analyses specific effects — word order, tense choice, enjambment, rhetorical structure. The analysis explains HOW the language creates excitement, not just WHAT happens."

5. **h2: "Common Mistakes to Avoid"**
   - Unordered list:
     - "Retelling the story instead of analysing the language"
     - "Quoting Latin without translating it (examiners cannot assume you understand)"
     - "Identifying a device ('this is alliteration') without explaining its effect"
     - "Writing about the topic (e.g., Roman battles) instead of the author's technique"
     - "Starting a new PETE paragraph with only 2 minutes left — finish what you have instead"
